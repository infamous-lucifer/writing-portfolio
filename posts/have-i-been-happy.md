**Have I ever really been happy?**  
Or have I just been in love with grief?

I think grief might be the only thing  
that truly knows how to stay.

I don’t have to chase it.  
I can find it.  
I can love it.  
I can hold it —   
and it holds me back.

Grief feels like the most real emotion I know.  
It’s definite.  
It’s certain.  
It’s loyal.

It doesn’t hide or pretend.  
It never demands proof or performance.

Maybe that’s why I trust it.

You see grief as something tied to what’s lost —   
a person, a dream, a moment that slipped away.

But I’m not talking about the cause.  
I’m talking about the feeling itself —   
the weight, the stillness, the ache.

The moment after collapse,  
when the world stops spinning  
and you’re just there,  
breathing.

That’s what I’ve fallen in love with.

Grief is freeing.  
It lets me break without punishment.  
It lets me need without apology.  
It lets me let go.

For once, collapsing doesn’t feel like failure.  
It feels like relief.

Grief wrings out the shame  
and leaves gentleness in its place.

You’ve spent a lifetime  
holding your breath for approval.  
Grief says:

> _“Exhale.”_

You’ve spent years  
hiding your fragility behind ambition, dreams, survival.  
Grief says:

> _“Come undone. Nothing bad will happen.”_

You’ve carried shame  
for being too much, too needy, too sensitive.  
Grief says:

> _“No. You are human. Let me hold you.”_

If grief lets you drop the mask,  
what would it take to feel that same safety  
In joy?  
In stillness?  
In love?

But joy is fragile —   
a flicker in a fragile glass.

It demands nothing —   
yet expect it, and it dims.  
Chase it, and it shatters.  
Hold it, and it slips.

Grief, though?  
Grief stays.  
Grief wants to be held.

Still, I have to ask —   
with reverence, no pressure:

Would you allow yourself to taste joy  
when it arrives on its own?

Not grabbing.  
Not begging it to stay.  
Not demanding permanence.

Just receiving it —   
like a soft, brief guest.

Can joy be a moment,  
while grief remains your home?

The pain isn’t just the loss.  
It’s the shame that follows:

> _“You let yourself believe. You fool.”_

So now you say:

> “If I never hope, I never break.”  
> “If I stay with grief, I never lose face.”  
> “If I never taste joy, I’ll never be humiliated when it leaves.”

You call this wisdom.  
Maybe it’s just protection.

So let’s speak plainly:  
Grief is honest.  
Hope is dangerous.  
Joy is fleeting.

And shame waits  
behind every moment you dare to want more.

But what if hope didn’t need to succeed?  
What if hope was a gesture —   
a small flame in a dark room  
that whispers:

> _“I still want something.  
>  Even if I don’t get it.”_

If that hope dissolves,  
let it melt into grief —   
not shame.

As Grief will hold you.  
Shame will punish you.

And grief will say:

> _“You tried.  
>  And I’m still here.”_
