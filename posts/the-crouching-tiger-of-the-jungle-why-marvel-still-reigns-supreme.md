</figure><p><em>Stunning, wasn’t it? Watching the planet quake and beat like a frantic heartbeat as Galactus consumed it whole, inside out. The sheer cosmic spectacle was breathtaking. Yet, amidst that awe-inspiring visual feast, a nagging question persisted: why did the seemingly invincible Devourer of Worlds succumb so easily, abandoning his throne to face defeat on a lesser planet in The Fantastic Four: First Steps? The film offers no clear answer, a puzzling narrative choice, especially when comic lore provides several compelling reasons why Galactus’s power should have remained unassailable.</em></p><p>This sentiment of perplexing narrative choices, however, extends beyond a single film. Everywhere you look, from news reports to YouTube reviews and critical assessments, the narrative persists:</p><blockquote><em>“superhero fatigue” is real, the genre is dead, it’s not “real art,” and even their own actors are supposedly longing for Oscar-worthy roles.</em></blockquote><p>The industry has seemingly worked against the Marvel Cinematic Universe (MCU), attempting to demystify it and compete with its colossal shadow. Yet, with every new MCU release, these very same voices remain nervous, anxiously searching for signs of failure, only to inadvertently keep the conversation alive and the public deeply interested. The irony is palpable: fans, driven by a desire to see if the MCU makes a comeback or if the naysayers finally win, flock to theaters. This dynamic has cemented Marvel’s position as a perennial chart-topper, dominating box office mountains year after year, while others may only taste such heights once in a lifetime.</p>
