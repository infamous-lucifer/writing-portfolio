</figure><p>I think my real problem is thinking about it — <br>too much.<br>Love.<br>Friendship.<br>What I don’t have.<br>What I fear I’ll never get.</p><p>But when I look at my life — <br>really look at it — <br>I’m doing okay.<br>I’m moving. Working. Learning. Showing up.<br>Maybe not shining in every area,<br>but I’m not collapsing either.</p><p>So why does this one area — <br>love, connection, companionship — <br>get to define everything?</p><p>Maybe the thinking itself is the trap.<br>The constant inner debates.<br>The association with social anxiety,<br>with labels, with pain and stories<br>I keep retelling until they become cages.</p><p>What if I chose something else?</p><p>What if I stopped trying to fix what’s missing<br>and started noticing what’s whole?</p><p>What if — even if it felt fake at first — <br>I wrote about the things I like about myself?</p><p>What if I named the parts that make me proud,<br>even if they sound like bragging?<br>Even if the voice inside calls it “narcissistic”?</p><p>Because maybe it’s not.<br>Maybe it’s repair.</p><p>Maybe I need a story where I’m not just<br>the wounded boy<br>but the man who’s trying.<br>The man who’s more than pain.<br>The man who is building something — <br>even if he still breaks sometimes.</p><p>And maybe…<br>maybe that version deserves a voice, too.</p>
